3 MACCABEES

CHAPTER 1

Now Philopater, on learning from those that came back that the places which belonged to himself had been taken by Antiochus, gave command to all his army, both footmen and horsemen, and took with him his sister Arsinoe, and marched out unto the parts of Raphia, where Antiochus and his forces were pitched. 2But a certain Theodotus, intending to accomplish his design, took with him the bravest of the armed men which had been before committed to his trust by Ptolemy, and crossed over by night unto the tent of Ptolemy, to kill him on his own responsibility, and thereby to end the war. 3But Dositheus, called the son of Drimylos, by birth a Jew, which afterward was apostate from the laws and customs of his country, conveyed Ptolemy away, and made an obscure person to lie down in his stead in the tent; and so it befell this man to receive the vengeance which was meant for the other.

4A fierce battle then took place, and the fight going well for Antiochus, Arsinoe continually went up and down the ranks; and, with hair dishevelled, with tears and entreaties, begged the soldiers to fight manfully for themselves, their children and their wives, and promised that if they proved victorious, she would give them two minas of gold apiece. 5And it thus fell out that their enemies were defeated in hand-to-hand encounter, and many of them were taken prisoners also. 6And having prevailed against this plot, the king then decided to proceed to the neighbouring cities, and encourage them. 7By so doing, and by distributing gifts to their temples, he inspired his subjects with confidence.

8The Judeans also sent some of their senate and of their elders to greet and to convey gifts; and they congratulated him on the falling out of events, which filled him with the greater eagerness soon to visit the city. 9Having arrived at Jerusalem, he sacrificed, and offered thank-offerings to the Greatest God, and did whatsoever else was suitable to the place. And upon entering the inner court, 10he was struck with the beauty and excellence; and admiring also the good order of the temple he conceived the purpose of entering the sanctuary itself. 11And they told him that this was not to be permitted, since even citizens of their own nation were not permitted to enter; no, nor even the priests in general, but only the supreme high priest of all, and he only once a year. Nevertheless, he would by no means give way. 12Even when the law was read to him, he persisted in obtruding himself, exclaiming that he ought to be permitted and saying, ‘Be it that others were deprived of this honour, I ought not to be’. 13And he put the question, ‘Why, when he entered all temples, none that were present forbade him?’ 14He was heedlessly answered by one, that he did wrong to boast of this. 15‘Well, be the cause what it may’, said he, ‘shall I not enter, with or without their consent?’ 16And the priests fell down in their sacred vestments, imploring God Most High to come and help in present time of need, and to avert the impulse of the fierce transgressor. And as they filled the temple with lamentations and tears, 17then those that had been left behind in the city were affrighted, and rushed forth, uncertain of the event. 18Virgins, which had been shut up within their chambers, came out with their mothers, scattering dust and ashes on their heads, and filling the streets with groanings. 19Women but newly separated off, left their bridal chambers, as well as the reserve that befitted them, and ran about the city in a disorderly manner. 20Newborn babes were forsaken by their mothers, and the nurses that waited upon them, some here, some there, in houses or in fields, as the women now, with an ardour which could not be checked, crowded into the most high temple. 21Various were the petitions that were offered up by them that assembled in this place, by reason of the unholy enterprise of Ptolemy. 22Along with these were certain of the citizens, who took courage, and were not content to see the king’s obstinacy, and his intention of carrying out his purpose. 23Calling out to arms, and to die bravely in defence of the law of their fathers, they created a great uproar in the place, and were with difficulty brought back by the aged and the elders to the station of prayer that they had occupied before. 24During this time the multitude kept on praying. 25Meantime, the elders which surrounded the king strove in many ways to divert his haughty mind from the design that he had formed. 26He, in his pride dismissed what was said, and went onward, with the view of carrying out this design. 27Yet even his own officers, when they saw these things, joined together with us in an appeal to Him, which hath all power to aid us in the present crisis, and not wink at such an overweening and lawless act. 28Such was the frequency and the vehemence of the cry of the assembled crowd that an indescribable noise ensued; 29for it seemed that not only them that were present, but even the very walls and floor sounded forth; inasmuch as all of them preferred dissolution rather than to see the temple defiled.

CHAPTER 2

Now it was that the high priest Simon bowed his knees over against the holy place, and spread forth his hands in reverent fashion, and uttered the following supplication: 2’O LORD, LORD, King of the heavens, and Ruler of the whole creation, Holy among the holy, sole Governor, Almighty, give ear unto us who are oppressed by a wicked and profane one, which is grown insolent in his presumption and strength. 3For Thou, the Creator of all things, and the Governor of all, art a righteous ruler, and judgest all those whose deeds are marked by pride and arrogance. 4Thou didst destroy the former workers of unrighteousness, among whom also were the giants, which trusted in their own strength and hardihood, by covering them with a measureless flood. 5Thou didst consume the Sodomites with fire and brimstone, who practised pride, and were distinguished among men for their wicked deeds, making them an example to after generations. 6Thou didst make known thy power when, with many and divers punishments, Thou didst try the bold Pharoah, when he enslaved Thy holy people Israel. 7For when he made pursuit after them, with chariots and with a multitude of soldiers, Thou overwhelmedst them in the depths of the sea, and gavest a safe passage unto those that put their trust in Thee, the LORD of all creation. 8These also that did witness the works of Thine hands, praised Thee, the Almighty One. 9Thou, O King, createdst the illimitable and measureless earth, and didst choose out this city. Thou didst sanctify this holy place for Thy name, albeit Thou needest nothing. Thou didst glorify it with Thy glorious presence, by establishing it to the glory of Thy great and honourable name. 10And Thou didst promise out of love toward the house of Israel that, should we suffer ills and distress overtake us, when we come into this house and pray, Thou wouldest hear our prayer. 11Verily Thou art faithful and true. 12And often when our fathers were hard pressed, Thou didst aid them in their low estate, and deliveredst them out of great evils. 13Behold now, O holy King, how through our many and great sins we are borne down and made subject to our enemies, and become powerless. 14In this our downfall, this bold and profane man seeketh to dishonour this Thy holy place, which is dedicated unto the name of Thy glory on the earth. 15Thy dwelling-place, the heaven of heavens, is indeed unapproachable to men. 16But since it seemed good to Thee to manifest thy glory among Thy people Israel, Thou didst sanctify this place. 17Punish us not by the uncleanness of their men, neither chastise us by means of their profanity, lest the lawless ones boast in their rage and say, 18‘“We have trodden upon the holy house as the houses that are idolatrous are trodden upon”. 19Blot out our iniquities, and do away our errors, and shew forth Thy tender mercy in this hour. 20Let Thy mercies speedily come upon us. Grant praise in the mouth of us who are cast down and broken in heart, and bring us peace.’

21At that time God, Which seeth all things, Who is beyond all Holy among the holy, heard that lawful prayer, and scourged the man that was greatly uplifted with pride and insolence. 22Shaking him this way and that, as a reed is shaken with the wind, He cast him upon the pavement powerless, with limbs palsied, by a righteous judgment deprived of the faculty of speech. 23His friends and bodyguards, beholding the swift recompense that had suddenly overtaken him, struck with exceeding terror and fearing that he would die, speedily removed him. 24Yet when in course of time he had come to himself, this severe check caused no repentance in him; but he departed with bitter threatenings.

25Now when Ptolemy proceeded to Egypt, he grew worse in his deeds of wickedness through his aforementioned companions in wine, men that were lost to all goodness. 26And not being satisfied with countless acts of indecency, his audacity so increased that he raised evil reports in the region; and many of his friends, watching his purpose attentively, joined in furthering his will. 27His purpose was to inflict a public stigma upon the Judean nation. Wherefore he erected a pillar at the porch of the tower, and graved the following inscription thereon: 28That entrance to their own temple was to be refused to all those that would not sacrifice; that all the Judeans were to be enrolled and reduced to menial status; that they that resisted were to be forcibly seized, and put to death; 29that they that were thus enrolled were to be marked upon their persons by the ivy leaf symbol of Dionysus, and to be set apart to their former status. 30But lest he should appear hateful to them all, he had it written beneath that if any of them should elect to enter the community of them that were initiated in the rights, they should have equal rights with the Alexandrians.

31Some there were that, outwardly abhorring the means of their maintaining godliness in the city, unhesitating gave in, and expected to derive some great honour from a future connection with the king. 32A nobler spirit, however, prompted the greater part to cling to their religious observances, and, by paying money that they might live unmolested, these sought to escape the enrollment; 33and cheerfully looking forward to future help, they abhorred their own apostates, considering them to be national foes and debarring them from the common usages of social intercourse.

CHAPTER 3

On discovering this, so incensed was the ungodly one, that he not only raged against the Judeans in Alexandria, but even more against them that lived in the country, and gave commandment that they should speedily be collected into one place, and most cruelly deprived of their lives. 2While these things were being done, a malicious rumour was uttered abroad against the Judean race by men who had banded together to injure them. The purport of their charge was that the Judeans kept others away from the ordinances of the law. 3Now the Judeans ever maintained their goodwill and unswerving loyalty toward the throne; 4yet, as they worshipped God, and observed His law, they made certain distinctions, and avoided certain foods. Hence they were held in odium by some persons; 5although, as they adorned their conduct with works of righteousness, they had established themselves in the good opinion of the world. 6What all the rest of mankind said was, however, made of no account by the Gentiles, 7who said much of the differences with regard to their worship and foods. They alleged that these men felt that they owed allegiance neither to the king nor his powers, but that they were hostile and opposed to the king’s interests. Therefore they attached no ordinary odium to them. 8The Greeks who lived in the city, who had suffered no harm of the Judeans, observed unexpected uproar and sudden conflux of people, and while to aid them was not in their power, since all was tyranny around, they nevertheless encouraged the Judeans by sympathizing with them in their troubles; and they expected a favourable turn of affairs, 9for such a community, which had done no wrong, could hardly be disregarded. 10Some of the neighbours, friends and fellow dealers of the Judeans, even drew them aside privily, pledged them their assistance, and promised to do their very utmost for them.

11Now Ptolemy himself, elated with his prosperous fortune, and not heeding the power of the supreme God, but thinking to persevere in his present purpose, wrote the following letter to the prejudice of the Judeans: 12‘King Ptolemy Philopater, to the commanders and soldiers in Egypt and in all places, greetings and health. 13I am right well, and so too are my affairs. 14Since our Asiatic campaign, the particulars of which ye know and which by the aid of the gods, not lightly given, hath been brought to a successful issue according to our expectation, 15we resolved, not with strength of spear but with clemency and much beneficence, as it were to nurse the inhabitants of Coelesyria and Phoenicia, and gladly to shew them kindness. 16So, having bestowed considerable sums of money upon the temples of the several cities, we proceeded even as far as Jerusalem, and went up to honour the temple of a sinful people, who never cease from their folly. 17To outward appearance they received us willingly, but they belied that appearance by their deeds. When we were eager to enter their temple, and to honour it with the most beautiful and exquisite gifts, 18they were so carried away by their old arrogance as to forbid us the entrance; while we, out of our forbearance toward all men, refrained from exercising our power upon them. 19And thus, exhibiting their enmity against us, they alone among the nations lift up their heads against kings and benefactors, as men unwilling to submit to any thing reasonable. 20We then, endeavoured to make allowance for the madness of these persons; and when we crossed over into Egypt, with victory we met treating all people with beneficence; as was befitting, so did we. 21Accordingly, we publicly declared amnesty toward their kinsmen at Jerusalem, and because of our connection with them, and the numerous matters that with sincere heart from a remote period were entrusted to them, we wished to venture a total alteration of their state, by bestowing upon them the rights of citizens of Alexandria, and admitting them to our customary sacred rites. 22All of this, however, they have taken in a very different spirit. With their innate malice, they have spurned the fair offer; and constantly inclining to evil, 23they have rejected the inestimable rights of citizenship. Not only so; but by using speech, and by refraining from speech, they abhor the few among them who are heartily disposed toward us, ever deeming that their ignoble course of proceeding will force us to do away with our reform. 24Wherefore, having then received certain proofs that these Judeans bear us every sort of ill will, we took precaution against the possibility of some sudden tumult against ourselves, lest we find these ungodly men to be traitors behind our back, and barbarous enemies. 25As soon, therefore, as soon as this letter come among you, in that same hour we command those Judeans that dwell among you, with wives and children, to be sent unto us, vilified and abused, in chains of iron, to undergo a death, cruel and ignominious, suitable to men disaffected. 26For when these men have been punished in one body, we perceive that our affairs for the future will be established on a firm and satisfactory basis. 27But whosoever shall shield a Judean, whether it be an old man, child or suckling, shall with his whole house be tortured to death. 28And whosoever is willing to inform, besides receiving the property of the person charged, shall be presented with two thousand drachmas from the royal treasury, shall be crowned with freedom. 29What place soever where a Judean is found sheltered, shall be made desolate, and burned with fire, and be altogether rendered useless to every living being for all time to come.’

30Such was the purport of the king’s letter.

CHAPTER 4

Wheresoever this decree was come, a feast at the public expense was made for the Gentiles with shouts of joy and gladness; as their long- held inveterate hatred were now made evident and outspoken. 2But among the Judeans there was great sorrow unceasing and cries of lamentation; whiles everywhere their hearts were set on fire, as they bewailed the sudden destruction that was decreed against them. 3What home or city or place at all inhabited, or what streets were there, which their condition did not fill with wailing and lamentation? 4For with such stern and pitiless spirit were they sent out one and all by the generals in the several cities, that the sight of the exceptional nature of their punishments moved even some of their enemies, who perceiving the common object of pity before their eyes and reflecting upon the uncertain issue of life, shed tears at this their miserable expulsion. 5A multitude of hoary-haired old men were led away, driven along with halting bending feet, urged onward by the impulse of a violent shameless force to a swift pace. 6Young women, who had lately entered the bridal chamber to enjoy the partnership of marriage, exchanged joy for misery, and with dust scattered upon their myrrh-anointed heads were carried away unveiled; and in the midst of outlandish insults, set up with one accord a lamentable cry in place of the marriage hymn, as they were torn by the barbarous cruelties of the Gentiles. 7Bound and exposed to public gaze, they were hurried violently on board ship. 8The husbands of these, in the prime of their youthful vigour, instead of crowns wore halters round their necks; instead of feasting and youthful mirth, they spent the residue of their nuptial days in wailings, and saw only the grave at hand. 9They were driven on board like wild beasts constrained by unyielding chains; of these some were fastened by their necks into the benches of the rowers, while the feet of others were enclosed in hard fetters. 10Moreover, the thick planks of the deck were above them; thus darkness covering their eyes on every side, they received the treatment befitting traitors during the whole voyage. 11They were conveyed to the place called Schedia; and with the journey now concluded according to the king’s commandment, he ordered that they be enclosed in the hippodrome before the city, which was built with a wall, immense in its compass, and well adapted by the situation thereof to make them an example to the gaze of all comers into the city, as well to them that went abroad into the country, that they could hold no communication with his forces nor in any way claim to be within the city walls. 12When this was done, the king, hearing that their Jewish brethren in the city often went out and lamented the melancholy distress of these victims, 13in his rage commanded that they should be carefully subjected to the same treatment as the others; neither should any of their punishment be omitted. 14He further commanded that the whole nation was now to be enrolled by name, not for that hard servitude of labour which we have a little before mentioned, but that he might expose them to the aforementioned tortures and finally, in the short space of a day, might extirpate them by his cruelties. 15The enrolling of these men was carried on with bitter haste and zealous diligence, from the rising up of the sun unto the going down thereof, and was not brought to an end when it ceased after forty days. 16The king was filled with great and constant joy, and celebrated feasts before his temple idols with a heart far from the truth, and with a profane mouth, giving glory to things that were deaf, and could not speak and incapable of aiding itself, and uttering unworthy speech against the Greatest God. 17But at the end of the aforementioned interval of time, the scribes brought word to the king that he would no more be able to accomplish the enrollment of the Judeans, because their number was too great. 18For they said that inasmuch as there were many still left in the land, of whom some were in inhabited houses and others scattered about in various places, therefore all the commanders in Egypt were not sufficient for the work. 19But the king threatened them, and charged them with taking bribes in order to contrive the escape of the Judeans; but was clearly convinced of the truth of what had been said, 20when they said and proved to him that the paper and pens they used had failed them. 21Now this was the activity of the unconquerable providence of Him Which assisted the Judeans from heaven.

CHAPTER 5

Then the king, being full of rage and altogether fixed in his furious design, called Hermon, which had charge of the elephants, 2and commanded that on the following day he drug all the elephants, being five hundred in number, with a plenteous quantity of unmixed wine, and handfuls of incense infused; and when they were maddened by the copious draughts of drink, to lead them in for the Judeans to meet their doom. 3When he had issued these commands, he went to his feasting, and gathered together all those of his friends and of the army who hated the Judeans the most. 4The master of the elephants, Hermon, fulfilled all that was commanded punctually. 5The servants that were assigned to the Judeans went out about eventide, and bound the hands of the miserable victims, and took further precautions for their security at night, thinking that the whole race would meet a final destruction. 6But the Judeans, whom the Gentiles believed to be destitute of all protection (for chains fettered them about), 7called upon the Almighty LORD, and ceaselessly besought with tears their merciful God and Father, Ruler of every power, 8to overthrow the evil purpose which was gone out against them, and to deliver them by a glorious manifestation from that fate which lay before them. 9And so their entreaty so earnest went up to heaven. 10Then Hermon, who had given his merciless elephants their drink, so that they were filled with a great abundance of mingled wine and frankincense, came early to the palace to certify the king thereof. 11He, however, had sent upon the king a portion of sleep, that good creature, which from the beginning of time, hath been bestowed by night or by day by He that giveth freely to all whomsoever He willeth. 12And by the action of the LORD he was held fast by so pleasant and deep a sleep, that he quite failed of his unjust purpose, and was thus greatly cheated of his unflinching resolve. 13But the Judeans, having escaped the hour which had been fixed, praised their holy God, and again entreated Him Who is easily reconciled to display the power of His powerful hand to the overweening Gentiles. 14The middle of the tenth hour had well nigh arrived, when the guest master, seeing that they that were bidden were assembled, came and shook the king. 15And when he had with difficulty gained his attention, he told him that the mealtime was already passing, and opened the matter with him. 16The king considered this, and then turned aside to his drinking, and commanded them that were come to the banquet to sit down over against him. 17When this was done, he urged them to give themselves over to feasting, and to make this present portion of the banquet joyful by celebrating the more. 18After the feastings grew on, the king sent for Hermon, and inquired of him with fierce threatenings wherefore the Judeans had been suffered to outlive another day. 19But when Hermon shewed that he had done his bidding whiles it was yet night, being in this confirmed by his friends, 20the king then, with a barbarity exceeding that of Phalaris, said that the Judeans might have to thank his sleep of that day, ‘This night lose no time, and make ready without delay the elephants in the like manner, for the destruction of the lawless Judeans’. 21When the king had spoken, all those that were present with one accord readily and joyfully approved; and then each man returned to his own home. 22Nor did they employ the night in sleep so much as in contriving every kind of cruel mockery for those they thought to be doomed. 23The morning cock had just crowed, when Hermon, having harnessed the brutes, began to set them in motion in the great colonnade. 24The city crowds were collected together to behold this most pitiful spectacle and waited impatiently for the break of day. 25The Judeans, at their last breath, spread forth their hands and prayed the Greatest God in tearful supplications and mournful strains again to help them speedily. 26The sun’s rays were not yet shed abroad, and while the king received his friends, Hermon came to him, calling him out, and saying that what he desired was now in readiness. 27But he, upon receiving the report, was astonished at this unwonted invitation; and overwhelmed with a spirit of oblivion about every thing, inquired what was the object was of this hasty preparation for him. 28But this was the working of Almighty God; for He had put in the king’s mind a forgetfulness of all that he had previously purposed. 29Hermon and his friends pointed out the readiness of the animals and the forces: ‘They are ready, O king, at thine own strict intention’. 30But the king was filled with fierce anger at these words, for by the providence of God regarding these things, his mind had become entirely confused. With a harsh look he said, 31‘Thy parents or thy children, were they here, I should have furnished to be a large repast to the wild beasts, in the stead of these innocent Judeans, which have shewed a firm and full loyalty to me and my fathers. 32Had it not been for the affection from our common nurture and familiar friendship, and the usefulness of thine office, thy life should have gone for theirs.’ 33Hermon, suffering an unexpected and alarming threat, was troubled in visage, and fallen in countenance. 34The king’s friends also sullenly stole out one by one, and dismissed the assembled multitudes each to his own occupation. 35Then the Judeans, having heard of what the king had spoken, praised the manifest LORD God and King of kings, because they had obtained this help also from him. 36Now the king arranged another banquet after the same manner, and encouraged the guests with an invitation to mirth. 37And he summoned Hermon to his presence and said with threats, ‘How oft, O wretch, must I repeat my commands unto thee about these same persons? 38Once more arm the elephants against the morrow for the destruction of the Judeans.’ 39His kinsmen that were reclining with him at table wondered at his instability of mind, and thus reproached him: 40‘O king, how long dost thou make trial of us as of men bereft of reason, commanding now a third time their destruction, and then in turn recalling thy decrees. 41For this cause the city is in a tumult with expectation; it swarmeth with factions, and is continually on the point of being spoiled.’ 42The king, being in everything another Phalaris, and filled with madness, made no account of the changes of mind that had come to pass with him toward the deliverance of the Judeans, and sware a bootless oath that he would forthwith to send them to Hades, tormented by the knees and feet of the beasts; 43and that he would also invade Judah, and level the towns thereof with fire and the spear; and that he would burn to the ground that temple which he might not enter, and render it ever after void of those that offered sacrifices there. 44Joyfully his friends brake up together with his kinsmen, and faithfully arranged the forces in guard at the most convenient places of the city. 45And the master of the elephants, having brought the beasts into an almost maniacal state, by drenching them draughts of wine mingled with incense, and decking them with frightful instruments, 46about early morning entered the court - the city being now filled with an multitudes without number at the hippodrome - and called the king unto the business in hand. 47So he, filling his ungodly mind with rage, rushed about along with the beasts. With heart invulnerable he longed to see with the very apples of his eyes the sad and pitiful doom of the aforementioned people. 48But the Judeans, when they saw the elephants going out at the gate followed by the armed force, and when they saw the dust raised by the passing throng, and heard the loud cries of the crowd, 49thought that they had come to the last moments of their lives, the end of their most miserable suspense. They gave way, therefore, to lamentations and groanings; they kissed each other; those nearest of kin to each other hung about one another’s necks, fathers about their sons, mothers their daughters, and others with infants at their breasts, which drew their last milk. 50Nevertheless, when they considered the succour before received from heaven, they prostrated themselves with one accord, removed even the sucking infants from their breasts, 51and cried out to Ruler over every power to reveal Himself and have mercy upon them, as they now stood at the gates of Hades.

CHAPTER 6

Then a certain Eleazar, famous among the priests of the country, which had attained to length of days, and throughout his life had been adorned with every virtue, caused the elders which were about him to cease to cry out to the holy God, and prayed thus: 2‘O King, mighty in power, Most High, Almighty God, Who orderest the whole creation with Thy tender mercy, 3look upon the seed of Abraham, upon the children of the sanctified Jacob, the people of Thy sanctified inheritance, O Father, which is now being wrongfully destroyed as strangers in a strange land. 4Pharoah with his host of chariots, the former prince of this same Egypt, exalted with lawless insolence and loud-sounding tongue, didst Thou destroy with his proud army, drowning them in the sea, and shewed forth the beams of Thy mercy upon the race of Israel. 5When Sennacherib, the grievous king of the Assyrians, glorying in his countless hosts, had subdued the whole land with his spear, and was lifted up against Thy holy city, speaking words grievous to be heard, with boastings and insolence, Thou, O LORD, didst utterly break him, shewing forth Thy might unto many nations. 6When the three friends in the land of Babylon of their own will exposed their lives to the fire rather than serve vain things, Thou didst send a dewy coolness through the fiery furnace, and brought the fire upon all their adversaries. 7Daniel, who through envious slanderings was hurled unto lions down below the earth as a prey to wild beasts, Thou didst bring back again unharmed to light. 8And when Jonah was pining away in the belly of the sea-bred monster, Thou didst look upon him, O Father, and revealed him to all his kindred unscathed. 9And now, O Thou that hatest insolence, Thou that aboundest in mercy, Thou that art the protector of all things, shew Thyself speedily unto them of the race of Israel, who now suffer outrage by abhorred and lawless nations. 10If our life during our exile hath been caught with ungodliness, deliver us from the hand of the enemy, and destroy us, O LORD, by the death which Thou preferrest. 11Let not the vain-minded praise their vain things at the destruction of Thy beloved, saying, “Neither did their God deliver them”, 12Thou Who art All powerful and Almighty, O Eternal One, behold! Have mercy upon us, who are being withdrawn from life like traitors, by the unreasoning insolence of transgressors. 13And let the Gentiles shrink in fear of Thine invincible might today, O honourable One, Who hast power to save the race of Jacob. 14The whole band of infants and their parents with tears beseech Thee. 15Let it be shewn unto all the Gentiles that Thou art with us, O LORD, and hast not turned Thy face away from us; but as Thou saidst that Thou didst not despise them even in the land of their enemies, so do thou fulfil this saying, O LORD.’

16Now at the time that Eleazar had ended his prayer, the king came along to the hippodrome, with the wild beasts, and with all the pride of his power. 17When the Judeans saw this, they uttered a loud cry to heaven, so that even the valleys that were near by resounded with them, and caused a terror irrepressible throughout the army. 18Then the most glorious, all-powerful and true God shewed forth His holy countenance, and opened the gates of heaven; from whence two angels, dreadful of form, came down, visible to all but the Judeans. 19And they stood over against the host of the enemy, and filled them with confusion and dread, and bound them with immoveable fetters. 20And even the king began to shudder, and oblivion palsied his sullen insolence. 21Then the beasts turned back upon the armed forces, which followed upon them, and trod them down, and destroyed them. 22 23 The king’s wrath was changed into compassion for what he had devised.For when he heard the cry, and saw them all prostrate on the verge of destruction, with tears he angrily threatened his friends, saying, 24‘Ye are guilty of treason, and have exceeded tyrants in cruelty, and even me your benefactor do ye labour to deprive at once of my dominion and my life, by privily devising measures which are unprofitable to the kingdom. 25Who is it that hath removed each man from his home, and gathered together here those who had faithfully held the strongholds of the country? 26Who hath thus consigned to unmerited punishments those who in goodwill toward us from the beginning have in all things surpassed all nations, and who often have engaged in the worst of dangers to men? 27Loose, loose the unjust bonds, and send them to their homes in peace, begging their pardon for what before hath been done. 28Release the sons of the Almighty living God of heaven, Who from our forefathers’ time till now hath granted a uninterrupted and glorious stability to our affairs.’ 29These things he said, and they, released the same moment, having now escaped death, blessed God their holy Saviour.

30The king then departed to the city, and called the overseer of his revenues unto him, and bade him provide for the Judeans wine and all else needful for a seven days’ quantity of feasting. He decided that they should celebrate their deliverance with all joyfulness in the very place wherein they expected to meet with their destruction. 31Then they, which before were despised and nigh unto Hades, yea, rather advanced into it, partook of the cup of salvation, instead of bitter and lamentable death. Full of exultation, they parted out the place intended for their fall and burial to parties of revelry. 32Ceasing their miserable strains of woe, they took up the songs of their fathers, praising the Saviour and wonderworking God. All groans, all wailing were laid aside; they formed dances in token of peaceful joy. 33So also the king collected a number of guests for the occasion, and returned unceasing thanks lavishly for the unexpected deliverance that had been afforded him. 34And those that had believed that the Judeans would be destroyed, and be for carrion, and that had enrolled them with joy, groaned aloud, as they themselves now were clothed with shame, and had the fire of their courage ingloriously put out. 35But the Judeans, as we have just said, organized dancing; and they gave themselves up to feasting, glad thanksgivings and psalms. 36They made a public ordinance to commemorate these things for generations to come, as long as they should be sojourners. Thus they established these days as days of mirth, not for the purpose of drinking or luxury, but because God had saved them. 37They requested the king to send them back to their homes. 38They were being enrolled from the twenty-fifth of Pachon unto the fourth of Epiphi, a period of forty days; the measures taken for their destruction lasted from the fifth of Epiphi until the seventh, that is, three days. 39The Ruler over all did during this time manifest forth His mercy gloriously, and did deliver them all together unharmed. 40They feasted upon the king’s provision up to the fourteenth day, and then asked to be sent away. 41The king commended them, and wrote the subjoined epistle, of magnanimous import for them, to the commanders of every city:

CHAPTER 7

‘King Ptolemy Philopater unto all the commanders throughout Egypt, and unto all that are set over affairs, greeting and good health. 2We ourselves, and our children, are well; and God directeth our affairs according to our desire. 3Certain of our friends did of malice vehemently urge us to gather the Judeans of our realm in a body, and to punish them as traitors with monstrous punishments. 4For they declared that our government would never be firmly established, till this was accomplished, since they bear enmity toward all the nations. 5They also brought them down cruelly fettered in grievous chains as slaves, nay, as traitors and without enquiry or examination endeavoured to destroy them. They girded themselves with a savage cruelty more savage than Scythian custom. 6For this cause we on our part severely threatened them; yet according to the clemency we bear unto all men, we barely spared their lives. And finding that the God of heaven surely protecteth the Judeans, and that he taketh their part as a father for his children, 7and taking into consideration their friendship and goodwill which they had toward us, and toward our fathers, we have as we ought acquitted them of every sort of charge. 8And we have instructed all of them to return unto their several homes, with none doing them wrong or reproaching them for what has come to pass contrary to reason. 9For know ye that should we conceive any evil design against or in any manner aggrieve them, we shall ever have as our opposer not man, but He that is sovereign over every power, God Most High, from Whom there will be no escape, in everything as the avenger of such deeds. Fare ye well.’

10When they received this letter, they were not forward to depart straightway, but petitioned the king, that at their own hands should those of the race of the Judeans that had willingly transgressed against the holy God and the law of God receive the punishment they deserved. 11For they declared that they, that had for their bellies’ sake transgressed the ordinances of God, would never be favourably disposed toward the affairs of the king. 12The king then admitted the truth of this reasoning, and consented and granted unto them a general licence, without royal authority or supervision, freely to destroy those that everywhere in the kingdom had transgressed the law of God. 13They then applauded him as was fitting, and their priests and all the multitude shouted the Alleluia. They then joyfully departed. 14Then they punished and destroyed with ignominy every polluted Jew that fell in their way, 15slaying thus in that day above three hundred men; which day they celebrated as a day of joy, seeing they had destroyed them that were profaned. 16But those who had held fast their God even unto death, and had received the full enjoyment of deliverance, departed from the city, garlanded with sweet flowered wreaths of every kind, and giving thanks unto the one God of their fathers, the eternal Saviour of Israel, in words of praise and melodious hymns.

17Having arrived at Ptolemais, called from the feature of that district ‘Rose- bearing,’ where the fleet, in accordance with the general wish, waited for them seven days, 18they celebrated their deliverance; for the king had generously granted them severally the means of securing a return home. 19They landed in peace with fitting thanksgiving, and they determined to keep these days during their sojourn as days of joyfulness. 20Then, having inscribed them as sacred upon a pillar, and dedicating the place of their festivity to be one of prayer, they departed unharmed, free, abundant in joy; since by the king’s commandment they had been brought safely by land, and by sea, and by river, each to his own home. 21They had more weight than before among their enemies, and were honoured and feared; and they were not subject to confiscation of their goods by any man. 22Besides they all received back each man his own, according to the enrollment, those that had obtained their goods giving them up with the greatest terror. For the supreme God wrought with perfectness mighty works for their deliverance. 23Blessed be the Redeemer of Israel throughout ages everlasting. Amen.
